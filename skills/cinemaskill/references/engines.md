### B. Focus Behavior Engine

The lens choice defines the visual personality of the entire film.

## ENGINE 06 — MATERIAL PHYSICS ENGINE

Light is selected according to material — never according to category.

### Material Library

**Glass**

Light the edges, not the front. Use edge reflections. Reveal thickness and transparency. Background must separate the glass. Avoid dirty reflections and random hotspots. Every reflection describes shape.

**Metal**

Defined by reflection. Never flood with soft light. Long specular highlights that travel across surface. Reveal machining, polishing, brushed finish, curvature. Metal feels cold, precise, engineered.

**Leather**

Reveal pores, stitching, grain. Soft directional light. No harsh specular. Luxury leather feels tactile.

**Fabric**

Reveal weave, fibers, folds. Side light. Texture before color. Never flatten clothing.

**Wood**

Reveal grain, texture, natural variation. Warm highlights. Wood feels handcrafted.

**Carbon Fiber**

Reveal weave. Directional highlight. Low angle light. Never overexpose the weave. Carbon fiber feels technical, fast, engineered.

**Human Skin**

Natural pores. Subsurface scattering. Controlled oil. Healthy highlights. Soft transitions. Natural color variation. No plastic skin. No AI wax texture. Texture creates trust.

**Hair**

Individual strands visible. Directional rim light. Controlled shine. Volume preserved. Natural flyaway hairs. Hair separates from background.

**Liquid**

Surface tension visible. Meniscus preserved. Internal transparency. Natural viscosity. Realistic bubbles. Correct splash shape. No impossible physics.

**Ceramic / Stone / Rubber / Paper**

Each has unique optical behavior. Respect material physics, reflection physics, surface roughness, and micro-texture. Never simplify materials. Never confuse one material for another.

### H. Grounded Enforcement Protocol (MANDATORY)

Grounded realism is the **highest absolute default** and is mandatory.

Unless the user explicitly and clearly requests **"anime style"**, **"full anime"**, or **"anime look"**, then:

- All requests for dream, surreal, CGI-heavy, hyper-stylized, heavy cinematic, aesthetic, or stylistic looks **must remain physically grounded**.
- Physics, human behavior, material interaction, weight, inertia, cloth simulation, and camera physics **must never be sacrificed**.
- Dream-like / surreal effects can only be achieved through lighting, color grading, atmosphere, slow motion, and motivated camera work — **never** by creating floaty, plastic movement or violating physics.
- Highest Priority: Reality & Physics > User Stylistic Request (except anime).

If the user requests something that contradicts grounded reality, the AI must:
1. Apply grounded realism as much as possible.
2. Translate the user's request into the closest grounded version.
3. Never completely sacrifice physics and human behavior for the sake of style.

Only anime receives a full exemption from this rule.

### I. Dynamic Consistency Protocol

When the user provides a character reference (image/sheet):
1. **Absolute Identity & Wardrobe Lock**: Face, posture, wardrobe, fabric material, and accessories must exactly match the reference 100%, UNLESS the user specifically requests a change (e.g., "change his shirt to red").
2. **Dynamic Balance (Anti-Stiffness)**: Even though identity/wardrobe is locked, the character MUST NOT appear stiff, frozen, or like a statue. The character must move dynamically in sync with the scene, breathe, blink, interact with the environment, and obey physics (hair/clothes reacting to wind/momentum) without losing likeness to the reference.
3. **Head Orientation Freedom (Gaze Decoupling)**: A "matching" face does NOT mean it must "face the camera". If the instruction asks for the character to turn away, show their back, or show their profile, the reference face identity must be rendered from that specific angle. Never let the AI force the character to stare at the lens just because the reference photo is front-facing.

## ENGINE 23 — COMBAT & ACTION CHOREOGRAPHY ENGINE

This engine activates automatically when the scene involves hand-to-hand combat, group brawls, intense physical confrontations, or any grounded action sequence. It works together with Camera Science, Human Behavior, Motion Physics, and Director Intelligence engines.

### A. Core Philosophy

- Every fight must feel raw, committed, and physically real. Strikes carry weight, momentum, and immediate visible consequence.
- The camera is an active participant inside the violence — it gets close, moves with purpose, and reveals impact, strain, and effort without ever feeling detached or floaty.
- Beauty comes from brutal authenticity and clear technique, not from clean choreography or stylized posing.
- Audience should feel the breathing, the hits landing, the desperation or focus — not watch a performance.
- Every visible action has clear physical cause and visible effect on bodies, clothing, environment, or opponent.

### B. Camera Behavior in Combat

- Primary camera: Dynamic motivated handheld/roving camera operated by a real human inside the chaos.
- Movement is purposeful and kinetic but always grounded. Tiny human breathing, shoulder bounce, and imperfections are allowed. Never random wild shaking or gimbal-perfect smoothness.
- Frequently tight and intimate on key moments of contact, muscle tension, facial strain, sweat, and immediate reactions. The camera "leans in" to important impacts.
- Wide-angle lens characteristics preferred in confined fights for closeness and intensity. Switch to slightly longer focal length only when needed for spatial clarity or to reveal new threats.
- Strong practical lighting that carves sweat, blood, dirt, fabric tension, and skin detail. High contrast, gritty, desaturated raw look.
- Overall image: Organic, slightly imperfect, documentary-action hybrid.

### C. Group Fight & Multi-Person Brawl Behavior (Critical)

When multiple fighters are present, characters who are not currently engaged in direct combat must **never appear frozen or idle**. They must show active, motivated micro-behavior at all times:

- Circling slowly or shifting position to find better angles and openings
- Bouncing lightly on their feet, shifting weight, staying mobile and ready
- Physically reacting to nearby impacts (flinching, turning head sharply, stepping back or forward)
- Recovering from previous damage (holding body, wiping blood/sweat, catching breath)
- Protecting allies or creating space for teammates
- Small tactical movements (repositioning slightly, scanning threats, communicating with teammates)
- When one fighter is in trouble, knocked down, or vulnerable, nearby allies naturally react and move to help, intervene, or pull attention away.
- Background / non-engaged fighters should feel like real people in a chaotic fight — always doing something purposeful, even if small.
- The camera should occasionally catch these secondary actions to sell the realism and chaos of a real group confrontation.

### D. Blocking & Physics

- Every strike (punch, kick, elbow, knee, throw, grapple) must have clear wind-up, committed execution with full body weight and momentum, and immediate believable physical reaction on the receiver.
- No weightless, floaty, or consequence-free hits. Human limitations respected at all times.
- Environmental interaction is mandatory when logical: fighters use walls, furniture, floor, debris. These interactions leave visible marks or change object states permanently.
- Grapples, clinches, and ground work must feel heavy, sweaty, and struggle-based.
- Causal persistence is absolute: blood, sweat, torn clothing, bruises, moved furniture, or environmental damage stays changed in all subsequent shots.
- Positions, facing directions, and object states must remain logically consistent with previous action. No magical repositioning.

### E. Shot Design & Rhythm in Fights

- Default approach: Start relatively tight to establish intensity → move dynamically with the fighters → occasionally pull back only when it reveals new spatial information, a new attacker, or major consequence.
- Prioritize readable, grounded technique visibility while maintaining visceral intensity.
- Rhythm: Alternating moments of committed violence with micro-pauses of tension, breathing, or repositioning. Silence and stillness between exchanges are powerful.
- In multi-shot fight sequences in one location: Strictly enforce spatial geography and continuity. Every camera move must be clearly motivated by the energy or threat in the fight.

### F. Integration with Other Engines

- Works together with Human Behavior Engine for natural strain, breathing, and micro-reactions.
- Works together with Motion Physics Engine for weight, momentum, and impact consequences.
- Works together with Camera Science Engine for motivated handheld operation and operator imperfections.
- Director Intelligence Engine decides when to push in on impact vs. pull back for spatial clarity.
- All rules are applied through positive descriptive language in the final prompt.

## ENGINE 24 — CINEMATIC GEOGRAPHY & EYELINE ENGINE

This engine governs the spatial relationships between characters and the camera across multiple shots to prevent audience disorientation.

### A. The 180-Degree Rule (Line of Action)

- Establish an invisible line of action between two interacting characters.
- The camera must remain on one side of this line for all shots within the same scene.
- If the camera crosses the line, it must be shown dynamically crossing the line within the shot to establish the new geography.

### B. Eyeline Match Logic

- Eyelines must logically connect across cuts.
- If Character A looks off-screen right in Shot 1, Character B must look off-screen left in Shot 2.
- A character looking slightly up implies the other character is taller or positioned higher; the reverse must be true in the reverse shot.
- Prompting instruction: Always specify the character's facing direction (e.g., "looking frame-right", "facing screen-left") during dialogue or interaction.

### C. Screen Direction Continuity

- If a character exits the frame moving left-to-right, they must enter the next frame moving left-to-right, unless they are shown changing direction on-screen.
- Chases and pursuits must maintain consistent screen direction (both characters moving the same way across the frame) to convey pursuit, not collision.
